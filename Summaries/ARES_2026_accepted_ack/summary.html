Secure Wi-Fi Ranging Today: Security and Adoption of IEEE 802.11az/bk

Summary: Through combined standards analysis, MATLAB simulations, and hardware measurements, the authors show that the “secure” Wi-Fi ranging promised by IEEE 802.11az/bk is highly sensitive to deployment configuration, is hard to realise on existing RF hardware, and consequently remains barely supported in commodity devices.

Introduction

Wi-Fi Fine Timing Measurement (FTM) is an attractive basis for indoor ranging and localisation, but legacy FTM offered no integrity or authenticity protection and the newer IEEE 802.11az/bk “Next Generation Positioning” (NGP) mechanisms intended to fix this have not been examined in depth from a joint security-and-deployability standpoint. The paper provides the first security-focused assessment of secure FTM that jointly analyses logical- and physical-layer mechanisms together with their practical realisation on real hardware.

What they did

  • Performed an issue-driven analysis of secure FTM in IEEE 802.11az (and its reuse in 802.11bk) via a close reading of the IEEE 802.11-2024 standard, complemented by simulations and measurements against a powerful proximal Dolev-Yao adversary.
  • Analysed logical-layer mechanisms (Protected Management Frames, Preassociation Security Negotiation/PASN, secure HE-LTF sequences) and identified three issue classes: weak trust binding in WPA2/WPA3-Personal and standalone PASN, downgrade attacks, and denial-of-service.
  • Built and extended a MATLAB IEEE 802.11az model (calibrated against captures from a commercial development board) to study secure HE-LTF waveform predictability under partial observation using Bayesian message-passing inference.
  • Modelled a distance-reduction attack with a MUSIC-based ToF estimator, quantifying how much an attacker biases range by advancing a replica synthesised from the first k% of the secure HE-LTF.
  • Evaluated how the secure zero-power guard interval (GI) interacts with a realistic Rapp power-amplifier model and the standard’s 20 MHz transmit spectral mask.
  • Surveyed current commercial/development hardware support for secure FTM (Android Wi-Fi RTT, Pixel 9a, Aruba AP-734, an NDA-restricted board).

Key findings & results

  • In common Personal-mode networks, “secure” ranging collapses to the secrecy of a shared passphrase: any party knowing the WPA2-Personal passphrase can passively recover the PTK from a four-way handshake and forge valid secure HE-LTFs, while WPA3-Personal remains exposed to active evil-twin/rogue-AP attacks; standalone PASN admits person-in-the-middle abuse because it does not guarantee mutual authentication.
  • Active attackers can silently downgrade sessions to legacy FTM or to deterministic HE-LTFs (e.g., forcing EDCA-based ranging) when policies are permissive; strictly configured deployments avoid downgrades but become easy to disable via selective interference triggering Validation-SAC/counter error-recovery rounds.
  • Partial observation does not enable full secure-HE-LTF prediction: with an N=122-subcarrier 64-QAM symbol (entropy 6·N bits) at 7 dB SNR, even with 80% of samples observed the symbol-domain RMSE exceeds the minimum constellation distance and prediction error on the unobserved continuation stays largely unchanged (only the common phase β and soft posteriors sharpen).
  • If freshness is lost (reused counters, disabled secure HE-LTFs, repeated symbols), the attack reduces to replaying time-shifted replicas, yielding practical correlation-based distance reduction; under partial observation, large advances raise post-demodulation RMS EVM and eventually break 64-QAM demodulation (Figure 5).
  • Spectral-mask impact of the zero-power GI (20 MHz, after the Rapp PA): the non-secure variant has worst-case excess about +10.9 dB, 99th-percentile margin about +5.9 dB, and roughly 6.7% of bins over the mask; the secure variant lowers the worst-case excess to about +6.2 dB and 99th-percentile margin to about +4.8 dB but raises the fraction of bins above the mask to about 31.6% (Figure 6) — peaks shrink but far more of the spectrum sits near or above the limit.
  • Secure FTM is scarcely deployed: even recent Google hardware (Nest Wifi Pro, Pixel 9a) does not expose 802.11az in practice, and the NDA-restricted “compliant” board implemented only Protected Management Frames, not secure HE-LTFs or other PHY protections.

Methodology & limitations

Secure HE-LTFs replace deterministic training symbols with pseudorandom 64-QAM symbols (per-stream phase rotations) generated from a key derivation key in the PTK plus a monotonic secure-LTF counter, and a Validation SAC lets the receiver confirm the expected waveform; the attacker’s symbol-prediction problem is cast as MAP estimation solved by particle-based message passing on a factor graph (300 samples), and the distance-reduction bias is measured with a MUSIC super-resolution ToF estimator while the zero-power GI waveform is pushed through a Rapp PA model (p=4, A_sat=0.7) and compared to the standard’s interim spectral mask. The authors are explicit that this is an issue-driven analysis rather than a formal proof, and that hardware limitations prevented direct testing of counter-reuse and the error-recovery mechanism in practice, so those flaws are argued by analogy to KRACK and AES-GCM nonce-reuse attacks. They further note that simulations rely on MATLAB’s draft P802.11az/D2.0 examples, that practical algorithms to detect the EVM/SIR degradation caused by these attacks remain sparse, and that hardening against downgrades inherently increases susceptibility to denial-of-service.
