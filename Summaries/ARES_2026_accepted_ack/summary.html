Secure Wi-Fi Ranging Today: Security and Adoption of IEEE 802.11az/bk

Summary: A combined logical- and physical-layer security analysis of the finalised IEEE 802.11az/bk “secure” Wi-Fi ranging mechanisms shows that, under common deployment configurations and real RF hardware, their promised guarantees largely fail to materialise, explaining why secure ranging remains barely deployed.

Introduction

Wi-Fi Fine Timing Measurement (FTM) underpins indoor ranging and proximity-based services, but legacy FTM (IEEE 802.11mc) sends timestamps in the clear and uses deterministic training fields, leaving it open to distance manipulation. The 2023 IEEE 802.11az “Next Generation Positioning” amendment (and the bandwidth-extending 802.11bk) adds MAC- and physical-layer protections to fix this; this paper asks, via standards analysis, MATLAB simulation, and hardware measurements, how well those protections actually hold up in practice.

What they did

  • Performed an issue-driven security analysis of secure FTM by closely reading the consolidated IEEE 802.11-2024 standard, rather than a formal proof, under a powerful proximal Dolev-Yao adversary who knows the standard but cannot break crypto or physics.
  • Analysed logical-layer mechanisms: Protected Management Frames, Preassociation Security Negotiation (PASN), secure HE-LTF counters/Validation SAC, and ranging-mode negotiation, across WPA2/WPA3-Personal and PASN deployments.
  • Studied physical-layer security in a MATLAB IEEE 802.11az framework (MUSIC-based ToA estimation, draft P802.11az/D2.0), including secure HE-LTF predictability under partial observation via particle-based message-passing Bayesian inference, distance-reduction attacks, and zero-power guard-interval spectral behaviour with a Rapp power-amplifier model.
  • Surveyed real-world adoption and took measurements on a commercial development board (vendor redacted under NDA) and a Google Pixel 9a, finding the board implemented only Protected Management Frames, not secure HE-LTFs.
  • Released source code and artefacts at a public KU Leuven COSIC GitHub repository.

Key findings & results

  • In WPA2-Personal, any party knowing the shared passphrase can passively recover the PTK from an observed four-way handshake and forge valid secure HE-LTFs; WPA3-Personal resists passive recovery but still permits an “evil-twin” rogue AP because SAE proves only shared-password knowledge, not AP identity. Standalone PASN gives no mutual authentication and is open to person-in-the-middle relaying.
  • Permissive configurations allow silent downgrades: an attacker can block a protected FTM Request and forge an unprotected or EDCA-based one, dropping the session to legacy FTM or deterministic HE-LTFs; conversely, strictly requiring PTK + secure HE-LTFs hardens integrity but makes ranging trivially deniable via selective interference (DoS).
  • Predicting unobserved secure HE-LTF samples is an underdetermined problem with entropy H = 6·N bits (N non-zero subcarriers; N = 122 for 20 MHz); even with 80% of a symbol observed, symbol-domain RMSE exceeds the minimum constellation distance, so full-sequence recovery is infeasible — partial observation yields only soft information (the common phase β is recoverable, individual 64-QAM symbols are not).
  • If freshness is broken (counter reuse, disabled secure HE-LTFs, known keys), the attack collapses to replaying a time-shifted replica; even a weaker attacker observing only part of the sequence induces substantial distance bias against a MUSIC estimator, bounded only because large advances raise demodulation EVM and become self-defeating.
  • The zero-power guard interval worsens spectral-mask compliance: after the PA model, worst-case excess over the 20 MHz mask drops from ~+10.9 dB (legacy cyclic prefix) to ~+6.2 dB (secure), but the fraction of frequency bins exceeding the mask rises from ~6.7% to ~31.6% — peaks shrink but far more of the spectrum sits at or above the limit.

Methodology & limitations

The work combines standards reading with a MATLAB 802.11az simulation pipeline (waveform generation, MUSIC ToA/ToF estimation, particle message-passing with 300 samples at 7 dB SNR, and a memoryless Rapp PA model with p = 4, A_sat = 0.7) calibrated against captured board waveforms to validate the non-secure baseline. The authors are explicit that this is an issue-driven analysis, not a formal security proof. Key caveats: scarce NGP hardware meant the secure HE-LTF physical-layer protections and the counter-reuse error-recovery path could not be tested on real silicon (the only available board lacked them); attacks are demonstrated in simulation; and the development-board vendor is undisclosed under NDA, limiting reproducibility of the hardware portion.
